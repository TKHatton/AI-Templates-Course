Module 1

Understanding Actionable Information

Learn to distinguish between vague and actionable information, master the Decision-First Approach, and develop systematic methods for transforming AI outputs into clear, implementable insights that drive results.

1.1 Identifying Actionable Information

Instructions: Review each AI response below and determine whether it's actionable or vague. For vague responses, identify which elements are missing (specificity, context, or direction).

üìö Key Concept: The Three Elements of Actionable Information

Specificity: Concrete metrics, numbers, or clear descriptions

Context: Comparison to benchmarks, past performance, or industry standards

Direction: Clear recommendations for next steps or actions to take

Example:

AI Response: "Your marketing campaign is performing well."

Analysis: Vague. Missing all three elements:

  • Specificity: No concrete metrics or numbers
  • Context: No comparison to past performance or benchmarks
  • Direction: No recommendations for next steps

Practice Exercise 1:

AI Response: "Your website traffic increased by 23% this month compared to last month, with the blog section showing the highest growth at 45%. Based on this trend, you should prioritize creating more blog content in the categories that are performing best: 'AI Templates' and 'Productivity Tips'."

Practice Exercise 2:

AI Response: "Customer satisfaction has decreased slightly."

1.2 Transforming Vague Information

Instructions: For each vague statement below, rewrite it to include all three elements of actionable information: specificity, context, and direction.

Example:

Vague: "Your blog is getting traffic."

Transformed to actionable: "Your blog traffic has increased by 35% this month (reaching 12,500 visitors), which exceeds the industry average growth rate of 15%. The 'AI Tools' category has shown the strongest performance with a 60% increase. Based on this data, allocate 40% of your content creation resources to AI-related topics for the next quarter, and consider creating a downloadable guide on AI tools to capture email leads."

Practice Exercise 1:

Vague: "Your podcast is growing."

Practice Exercise 2:

Vague: "Customer churn is a problem."

1.3 The Decision-First Approach

Instructions: Transform each vague request into a Decision-First prompt by identifying the specific decision to be made and the information needed to make it effectively.

üí° Decision-First Framework

Instead of asking "Tell me about X," ask "I need to decide Y. Please analyze X to show me Z."

This approach ensures every AI response connects directly to a decision you need to make.

Example:

Vague Request: "Tell me about our email marketing."

Decision to be made: Whether to change our email frequency or keep the current schedule.

Decision-First Prompt: "I need to decide whether to increase our email frequency from weekly to twice-weekly or maintain our current schedule. Please analyze our email marketing data to show: 1. Open rates and click-through rates by day of week and time of day 2. Comparison of engagement metrics between weeks when we sent one email versus weeks when we sent multiple emails 3. Subscriber growth and unsubscribe rates over the past 6 months 4. Industry benchmarks for email frequency in the SaaS sector 5. Specific recommendation on optimal email frequency based on our audience engagement patterns"

Practice Exercise 1:

Vague Request: "Tell me about our website performance."

Building Your First Templates

Master the four key components of effective templates, learn to identify and fill gaps in your prompts, and develop a systematic testing framework to ensure consistent, high-quality results.

2.1 The Four Template Components

Instructions: Learn to identify and build the four essential components that make templates effective and reusable.

üèóÔ∏è The Four Template Components

1. Context: Who you are, what you do, your situation

2. Purpose: The specific goal or decision you need to make

3. Structure: How you want the output organized

4. Parameters: The specific data, constraints, or variables

Example: Business Review Template

Context: "I'm a small business owner running an e-commerce store selling handmade jewelry."

Purpose: "I need to review my monthly performance to identify what's working and what needs adjustment."

Structure: "Please organize the analysis into: 1. Revenue trends 2. Top performing products 3. Customer acquisition channels 4. Key challenges 5. Recommended actions for next month"

Parameters: "Monthly revenue: $15,000, Orders: 120, Top products: Silver rings (40% of sales), Traffic sources: Instagram (60%), Google (25%), Email (15%)"

Practice Exercise 1: Identify Components

Read this template and identify each component:

"I'm a fitness coach who works with busy professionals. I need to create a 4-week workout plan for a client who wants to lose 15 pounds and can only work out 3 times per week for 30 minutes. Please structure this as: 1. Weekly overview 2. Daily workout details 3. Progression plan 4. Nutrition guidelines. Client details: 35 years old, beginner level, has dumbbells and resistance bands, works from home."

2.2 Identifying and Filling Template Gaps

Instructions: Learn to spot missing components in templates and transform weak prompts into powerful, comprehensive templates.

üîç Common Template Gaps

Missing Context: AI doesn't know your role, industry, or constraints

Vague Purpose: No clear goal or decision to be made

No Structure: Output format not specified

Incomplete Parameters: Missing key data or variables

Example: Gap Analysis

Weak Template: "Help me with my social media strategy."

Missing: Context (who are you?), Purpose (what decision?), Structure (how organized?), Parameters (what data?)

Strong Template: "I'm a freelance graphic designer targeting small businesses. I need to decide which social media platforms to focus on for the next quarter to generate more leads. Please analyze my options and provide: 1. Platform comparison (reach, engagement, cost) 2. Content strategy for each 3. Resource requirements 4. Expected ROI 5. Recommendation with reasoning. My current followers: Instagram (500), LinkedIn (200), Facebook (100). Budget: $300/month."

Weak Template: "I need help with my email marketing. Give me some ideas."

2.3 Template Testing Framework

Instructions: Learn to test and refine your templates to ensure they consistently produce actionable results.

üß™ The Template Testing Process

1. Decision Test: Does the output help you make a specific decision?

2. Action Test: Can you take concrete steps based on the response?

3. Consistency Test: Does it work reliably across different scenarios?

4. Completeness Test: Are all necessary elements included?

Example: Testing a Content Strategy Template

Template Output: "Focus on video content, post 3x per week, use trending hashtags."

Decision Test: ‚ùå Fails - Which platform? What type of videos?

Action Test: ‚ùå Fails - No specific steps or timeline

Refinement Needed: Add platform specification, content types, production timeline, and success metrics
