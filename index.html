Module 1

Understanding Actionable Information

Learn to distinguish between vague and actionable information, master the Decision-First Approach, and develop systematic methods for transforming AI outputs into clear, implementable insights that drive results.

1.1 Identifying Actionable Information

Instructions: Review each AI response below and determine whether it's actionable or vague. For vague responses, identify which elements are missing (specificity, context, or direction).

Example:

AI Response: "Your marketing campaign is performing well."

Analysis: Vague. Missing all three elements:

  • Specificity: No concrete metrics or numbers
  • Context: No comparison to past performance or benchmarks
  • Direction: No recommendations for next steps

Practice Exercise 1:

AI Response: "Your website traffic increased by 23% this month compared to last month, with the blog section showing the highest growth at 45%. Based on this trend, you should prioritize creating more blog content in the categories that are performing best: 'AI Templates' and 'Productivity Tips'."

Practice Exercise 2:

AI Response: "Customer satisfaction has decreased slightly."

1.2 Transforming Vague Information

Instructions: For each vague statement below, rewrite it to include all three elements of actionable information: specificity, context, and direction.

Example:

Vague: "Your blog is getting traffic."

Transformed to actionable: "Your blog traffic has increased by 35% this month (reaching 12,500 visitors), which exceeds the industry average growth rate of 15%. The 'AI Tools' category has shown the strongest performance with a 60% increase. Based on this data, allocate 40% of your content creation resources to AI-related topics for the next quarter, and consider creating a downloadable guide on AI tools to capture email leads."

Practice Exercise 1:

Vague: "Your podcast is growing."

Practice Exercise 2:

Vague: "Customer churn is a problem."

1.3 The Decision-First Approach

Instructions: Transform each vague request into a Decision-First prompt by identifying the specific decision to be made and the information needed to make it effectively.

Example:

Vague Request: "Tell me about our email marketing."

Decision to be made: Whether to change our email frequency or keep the current schedule.

Decision-First Prompt: "I need to decide whether to increase our email frequency from weekly to twice-weekly or maintain our current schedule. Please analyze our email marketing data to show: 1. Open rates and click-through rates by day of week and time of day 2. Comparison of engagement metrics between weeks when we sent one email versus weeks when we sent multiple emails 3. Subscriber growth and unsubscribe rates over the past 6 months 4. Industry benchmarks for email frequency in the SaaS sector 5. Specific recommendation on optimal email frequency based on our audience engagement patterns"

Practice Exercise 1:

Vague Request: "Tell me about our website performance."
